Nichiren set a precedent for Buddhist social activism centuries before its emergence in other Buddhist schools. The uniqueness of his teachings was his attempt to move Buddhism from the theoretical to the actualizable. He held adamantly that his teachings would permit a nation to right itself and ultimately lead to world peace.

Nichiren stressed the concept of immanence, meaning that the Buddha's pure land is to be found in this present world (shaba soku jakk≈çdo). Related concepts such as attaining enlightenment in one's current form (sokushin j≈çbutsu) and the belief that enlightenment is not attained but is originally existing within all people (hongaku) had been introduced by K≈´kai and Saicho several centuries earlier.

These concepts were based on Chih-i's cosmology of the unity and interconnectedness of the universe called Three Thousand Realms in a Single Moment of Life (ichinen sanzen).[99]

Nichiren advanced these concepts by declaring that they were actualizable rather than theoretical - cause and effect were simultaneous instead of linear.

Consequently, Nichiren consistently and vehemently objected to the perspective of the Pure Land school that stressed an other-worldly aspiration to some pure land. Behind his assertion is the concept of the nonduality of the subjective realm (the individual) and the objective realm (the land that the individual inhabits) which indicates that when the individual taps buddhahood, his or her present world becomes peaceful and harmonious. For Nichiren the widespread propagation of the Lotus Sutra and consequent world peace ("kosen-rufu") was achievable and inevitable and tasked his future followers with a mandate to accomplish it.[101][102]:‚Ää68‚Ää

He launched his teachings in 1253, advocating an exclusive return to the Lotus Sutra as based on its original Tendai interpretations. His 1260 treatise Rissh≈ç Ankoku Ron (Á´ãÊ≠£ÂÆâÂõΩË´ñ, On Establishing the Correct Teaching for the Peace of the Land) argued that a nation that embraces the Lotus Sutra will experience peace and prosperity whereas rulers who support inferior religious teachings invite disorder and disaster into their realms.

The eight limbs form a sequence from the outer to the inner.

As with the Yamas, Patanjali explains how and why each of the Niyamas helps in personal growth.

Pratyahara is drawing within one's awareness. 

It is a process of retracting the sensory experience from external objects. It is a step of self extraction and abstraction. Pratyahara is not consciously closing one's eyes to the sensory world; it is consciously closing one's mind processes to the sensory world. 

Pratyahara empowers one to stop being controlled by the external world, fetch one's attention to seek self-knowledge and experience the freedom innate in one's inner world.
Pratyahara marks the transition of yoga experience from the first four limbs of Patanjali's Ashtanga scheme that perfect external forms, to the last three limbs that perfect the yogin's inner state: moving from outside to inside, from the outer sphere of the body to the inner sphere of the spirit.

Dharana means concentration, introspective focus and one-pointedness of mind. 
Dharana, as the sixth limb of yoga, is holding one's mind onto a particular inner state, subject or topic of one's mind.

The mind is fixed on a mantra, or one's breath/navel/tip of tongue/any place, or an object one wants to observe, or a concept/idea in one's mind.

Fixing the mind means one-pointed focus, without drifting of mind, and without jumping from one topic to another.

7. DhyƒÅna

Dhyana is contemplating, reflecting on whatever Dharana has focused on. 

If in the sixth limb of yoga one focused on a personal deity, Dhyana is its contemplation. 

If the concentration was on one object, Dhyana is non-judgmental, non-presumptuous observation of that object.

If the focus was on a concept/idea, Dhyana is contemplating that concept/idea in all its aspects, forms and consequences. Dhyana is uninterrupted train of thought, current of cognition, flow of awareness.
Dhyana is integrally related to Dharana, one leads to other. Dharana is a state of mind, Dhyana the process of mind. Dhyana is distinct from Dharana in that the meditator becomes actively engaged with its focus. Patanjali defines contemplation (Dhyana) as the mind process, where the mind is fixed on something, and then there is "a course of uniform modification of knowledge".

Adi Shankara, in his commentary on Yoga Sutras, distinguishes Dhyana from Dharana, by explaining Dhyana as the yoga state when there is only the "stream of continuous thought about the object, uninterrupted by other thoughts of different kind for the same object"; Dharana, states Shankara, is focussed on one object, but aware of its many aspects and ideas about the same object. 

Shankara gives the example of a yogin in a state of dharana on morning sun may be aware of its brilliance, color and orbit; the yogin in dhyana state contemplates on sun's orbit alone for example, without being interrupted by its color, brilliance or other related ideas.

Savitarka, "deliberative"; the citta is concentrated upon a gross object of meditation, an object with a manifest appearance that is perceptible to our senses, such as a flame of a lamp, the tip of the nose, or the image of a deity.

about ourselves, others, and the world we live in.

People become used to certain habits and ways of thinking, even to the point of resisting change, and reacting with fear and anger to ways of life and perspectives that are different from their own.

"If a pickpocket meets a saint, they only see their pockets...
If you wear shoes, the entire Earth is covered in leather..."

For people that live life from a place of negativity and cynicism, every negative experience only confirms their views, and every positive experiences are minimized, rationalized, or hand-waved away.

Attempting to convince an atheist that God exists, or a pessimist (sorry, realist!) to be optimistic, is as difficult as showing a bored child all the things they could play with, as difficult as convincing a racist old man to travel to other countries, or telling a hurt and bitter woman to trust others again.

The other side of this coin is, that if you attempt to introduce negativity to a positive person, it doesn't really work. Positive experiences reinforce their belief, and negative experiences are minimized, rationalized, or hand-waved away...

To someone who believes in themselves, in others, in the world, in God, every waking moment is a sign, a reminder, 
a manifestation of a conscious or unconscious desire, an encouraging synchronicity.

Is there a meaning, a purpose, an order to the universe?
Or is it all just pointless, mechanistic chaos? 
Is it all coincidence, or are there no coincidences?

It's all up to you - either way, you're right. 
Your Perception is Reality!

If you're not happy with the way things are, then obviously things have to change, and in order to change our lives, we must begin to look at everything, especially ourselves, in a different light. 

When we change our perceptions and beliefs of what is possible, what we are capable of, what our potential could be, we elevate and expand the horizons of our own consciousness - and if we're lucky, we do the same for others.

Would you tell your younger siblings, your nieces and nephews, your children, that they are limited in what they can do, what they can be? Would you tell them that the world around them is on a fixed path, that nothing can change?

Would you tell them to be 'Realistic', whatever that means - or will you tell them the truth - that if they express themselves, honestly and without hesitation, that they can change Reality itself through effort and will?

Now... what do you tell yourself every day?

If you want to liberate yourself, you have to look within - there is nothing outside of you that will save you.

There is no person, no place, no thing - no book, no drug, no method or teaching - that will lead you to lasting peace and happiness, unless it inspires you to go within.

Of course, thoughts will never stop appearing - the nature and purpose of the Mind is to continually find and invent stimuli, to exist within a cycle of discovery-analysis-understanding, even if the reality it is exploring is entirely imagined and illusory. 


And, if we operate under the fallacy that, in order to become realized beings, we must cultivate 'Good' Thoughts and ignore and suppress our 'Bad' Thoughts, we will end up in an endless conflict against ourselves, one another, and the world around us.


Stop giving your thoughts, impulses, and opinions so much power.


Break your identification with your own thoughts, and you are close to achieving inner freedom.


As you become more Aware of this, you begin to identify less and less with your stream of consciousness, and more so with the Awareness itself.


With realization of your inner awareness, the ego slowly falls away, and you realize that validation, satisfaction, lasting peace can only come from within - it cannot be attained by any means.
